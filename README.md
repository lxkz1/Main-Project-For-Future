# The device should work for everyone. It doesn't.
*April 2026 · Post #001*

281 million international migrants. Over 700 million elderly people worldwide. And almost every device they own was designed by someone who assumed a baseline of digital literacy they never had reason to develop.

The result is a gap nobody talks about seriously. Not a gap in hardware. Not a gap in connectivity. A gap in usability — at the most fundamental level. People who cannot navigate the device itself never get to the information, the services, the help that lives inside it. The problem isn't that the internet doesn't have answers. It's that millions of people can't get to the question.

> An app doesn't fix this. Another service to find, download, and learn how to use doesn't fix this. The fix has to be deeper.

What I'm building is an AI layer integrated directly into the device experience — not on top of it. Something that is simply there the moment you turn the screen on. It doesn't wait for you to find it. It understands where you are on the device, what you're looking at, and what you probably need. It speaks your language as a default, not as a setting. It guides you through the device itself: notifications you can't read, settings you've never opened, apps that assume you already know how they work.

And beyond navigation — it handles the harder layer. The government letter you received and don't understand. The medical form that needs your insurance number. The bureaucratic process with seven steps that assumes you know what each one means. The AI doesn't send you to a FAQ. It walks you through it, in your language, at your pace, without making you feel like you should already know this.

This is built on Anthropic's Claude. Not as a default choice — as a deliberate one. The ability to reason through complex multilingual documents, maintain context across a long conversation, and respond to vulnerable users with accuracy and patience is not a nice-to-have for this product. It is the product. A less capable model doesn't get you 80% of the way there. It gets you a tool that fails exactly when someone needs it most.

> The technology to build this exists right now. Every month it goes unbuilt is a month that millions of people navigate alone — not because help is impossible, but because nobody made it a priority.

I'm 15 restarting coding from scratch — I learned a bit at 7 and forgot everything. My long-term goal is quantum mechanics at MIT. This project is where I begin, and I'm documenting everything: the research, the failures, the architecture decisions, the progress.

The commits will be rough. The vision won't move.
